On Mon, Mar 9, 2009 at 12:20 PM, Stephan Aßmus <superstippi at gmx.de> wrote:
>
> On some accounts, I can understand the reasoning behind releasing early
> and releasing often. KDE 4 being a good example. It basically forces a
> lot of people into being beta testers, which means the software has more
> exposure and the pressure to fix bugs is higher. However, the problem
> with the frequent release cycles and ever new features and rewritten
> components is that the software never ever feels like it's finally
> getting mature. It's in constant Beta. Add to that the "complain to
> upstream" phenomenon and it seems from just our discussion here that the
> balance is tipping in the mind of users.

I think the key is whether the user is choosing to participate in the
"beta testing" or not. If they choose to get the latest and greatest
for all kinds of neat new features (and therefore new bugs too), then
great, that is their choice. But it is totally different when people
are essentially forced into upgrading to something that breaks what
worked before. That is what seems to have been happening too much
lately in Ubuntu and other parts of the Linux world.
