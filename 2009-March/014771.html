Ryan Leavengood schrieb:
> On Mon, Mar 9, 2009 at 3:58 AM, Axel Dörfler <axeld at pinc-software.de> wrote:
>> I guess the main problem is not development alone, but poor QA in the
>> first place. I hope that we can beat them in that place, but it
>> requires numerous testers of updates before the actual release.
> 
> I definitely agree that QA is the key here, but I think we have a
> major advantage over any kind of Linux: Haiku is one cohesive system,
> primarily written by the same set of developers, and we tend to make
> sure everything works right together. Whereas Linux distros have to
> cobble together all kinds of different pieces to get a working desktop
> system. We also have a lot less code that could break. In addition it
> seems Linux developers don't care at all for backwards compatibility,
> I guess because "anyone can recompile their stuff when it breaks."
> Which of course is a silly idea for 99% of users. Luckily we don't
> think like that.

On some accounts, I can understand the reasoning behind releasing early 
and releasing often. KDE 4 being a good example. It basically forces a 
lot of people into being beta testers, which means the software has more 
exposure and the pressure to fix bugs is higher. However, the problem 
with the frequent release cycles and ever new features and rewritten 
components is that the software never ever feels like it's finally 
getting mature. It's in constant Beta. Add to that the "complain to 
upstream" phenomenon and it seems from just our discussion here that the 
balance is tipping in the mind of users.
